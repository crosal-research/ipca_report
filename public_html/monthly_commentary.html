1 Scenario Assessment

The political crisis blew off at worst possible juncture. Clearly, the momentum was building around a possible approval of the pension system reform, and confidence over the economy was accordingly growing. Unfortunately, however, none of these can be taken for granted any longer, since now the fate of the reform is all but clear, and the economy looks set to be hampered by the political uncertainty.

As a matter of fact, soon after the crisis was triggered, we lowered our GDP forecast for 2017E to 0.1% from 0.5%, while we are now advancing the 207E year-end Fx rate to hover about R$|US$3.40, as opposed to our previous forecast of R$|US$3.20. Moreover, we had revised marginally up our CPI inflation forecast to 4.1% from 3.9%, just to lower it back shortly after to 3.8% due to the recent downward adjustment of gasoline and energy prices. Finally, in light the of the Brazilian Central Bank's (BCB) recent communication we are revising our 2017E year-end Selic rate to 9.0%, implying cuts of 75pbs and 50bps in the next two meetings, and full stop.

Granted, any scenario revision at moment ought to be taken with a huge grain of salt. In the particular case of monetary policy, we acknowledge that uncertainties around our new call may be tilted to the downside, but, on the upside, there is an important tail risk that cannot be dismissed. In other words, on the one hand, we reckon deflating forces remain pervasive, but, on the other one, existing uncertainties over the fiscal policy long-term outlook call for a premium. Therefore, the fate of the pension reform will be key to determine which of these forces should prevail down the line, and whether our new call will prove to be too conservative or not.

On the global front, the outlook persists favorable towards the country. The end of Trump's rally has led US long-term rates to new lows, while Brazil terms of trade have remained high for historical standards. Finally, the confirmation of Macron's victory in France's presidential election has forestalled the growth of populism in developed economies, at least for now, and shattered fears over EU disintegration.

In short, Brazil is certainly to blame for its own mess and, as such, ought to find an escape route from its ailments. In this respect, our working hypothesis is that the end the political stalemate shouldn't happen overnight, but Temer's economic team should largely remain in place along the way. As for reforms, we consider that the labor reform may be approved still in 1H2017 as is, while the outlook for the pension reform seems much less clear cut.

In any case, we are not very hopeful the outcome of the trial on Dilma/Temer's campaign funding will be any silver bullet in terms of stirring a rapid resolution of the political crisis. Whatever way it goes, we are likely to witness a legal row that should only gradually lead to a more politically stable scenario, meaning, that it is likely to take months until we get there.

2 Global Economy

The end of Trump's rally and Macron's election were the main recent events. The global economy continues to show signs of concerted growth, favoring cyclical activities. Low rates and low risk premium continue to prevail.

The US growth outlook persists largely intact. The economy is close to full employment and standing roughly at about its potential, which is, as it widely acknowledge by now, below its historical levels. Meanwhile, risk aversion remains puzzling low, close to its historical low, despite the increasing political noise coming from within.

On the momentary policy, there has been little changes since last month. Signals have reinforced the thesis that Mr. Tump's fiscal agenda will need to be revised down for political reasons, while Fed's strategy to shrink its balance sheet remains to be seen. Consequently, our bet that the Fed should hike its baseline rate for another couple of 25bps this year remains on the money, in tandem with the decline in long rates after the lukewarm jobs report of May.

Elsewhere in the EU, Macron's victory in France's Presidential election has eased fears of growing populism in developed markets, which, in particular, could lead to further fragmentation of the EU. Now, the next stop will be German's general elections scheduled to September 24th, but risks over there look less extreme.

Overall, global growth remains on the positive camp. Similarly to the previous month, cyclicals are expanding above 2016's trend, led by emerging Asia, thus favoring global trade. In addition, in real terms, the yuan is clearly overvalued vis Ã  vis the 2015-2016 years, which has contributed to tame some of the protectionist rhetoric in the US.

In face of this benign global backdrop, the main fear on our radar screen is the political risk in US combined with extremely low risk aversion. Taken together, they raise worries of possible tail events, should the market decide to turn sour because of the any meaningful political setbacks in the US.

3 Brazil

The political bombshell of three weeks ago has set the country on bumpy road, with consequences to monetary policy and the economy at large. Long-term fiscal outlook is again on the radar screen.

1Q20107E GDP growth of 1.0%qoqsa has been welcomed, but failed to change our vision for the medium-term. This is because growth has been driven primarily by the agriculture sector, which is unlikely to prevail going forward. Also, as we have guessed, the surge in industrial activity in January and February were largely boosted by statistical artifacts and so short-lived. Hence, more likely than not, we should witness yet another quarter of subdued economic performance in 2Q2017, probably, on the negative camp.

In fact, we are wary of the nasty relationship between growth and volatility, which historically hasn't gone down too well. The channels through which this dynamics plays out are many-fold, in particular, via confidence but also through the financial markets. Fortunately, the external sector's conditions are quite favorable. Trade balance in March reached at 2.7% of GDP on the 12 months rolling basis, while terms of trade are above historical standards, as in exhibit \ref{fig:tt}. Therefore, the combination of lower global long-term rates, constructive global growth, and sounding external accounts have managed to curb the depreciation of the local currency, despite the surge in sovereign risk spreads since the crisis was triggered.

3.1 Fiscal policy and Politics: risks to fiscal consolidation

Soon after the market gave Brazil the benefit of the doubt in 2Q2016, the BRL strengthened, inflation came down and, in due time, the Brazilian Central Bank (BCB) started to lower rates. Behind it all, there was a new political coalition supporting a long-term fiscal adjustment based on public spending cap and, potentially, the pension system reform as its ultimate pillar. As it so happened, real interest rates came down, triggering a whole positive dynamics, in particular, lowering public debt service costs (see exhibit \ref{fig:cost}), in a classic case of the so-called expansionary-contraction.

Unfortunately, the spurn of the ongoing crisis have shaken the pillars of this new coalition, and, accordingly, has raised questions over this forward looking-based fiscal adjustment. In fact, we reckon this type of concern is not groundless, as recent events have suggested.

As a matter of example, on May 31 the Lower House approved a 15 years phase-out plan to end a network of State level subsidies. This sounded all well, were it not for government having conceded on key points, and thus seriously undermining the very credibility of the bill.

More specifically, while the original bill stated the subsidies would gradually wane over the years, now sizable chunk will remain as is till the end of the period. Therefore, the deal stoke by the government and congress have reduced part of the savings along the way, while making a sudden drop in the subsidies at the end of the 15 years period not very credibility.

A similar signal can be read from the recent re-shuffle of the BNDES' CEO. Although we yet don't know whether the existing policy to curb subsidized credit and focus primarily on project with social spillovers will be maintained, the management change does raise questions in this respect. After all, changes on this front could have a meaningful impact on monetary policy, since the latter has been able to take a more prominent role in reviving the economy, among other things, because of the shrinkage of earmarked credit.

In sum, the long-term type of fiscal adjustment is under suspicious, and should the government not reorganize its coalition soon, there are risks the pension system reform may be watered down, and the long-term debt-to-GDP dynamics may lose a key credibility anchor and be yet again under doubts.
